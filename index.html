hashes.

Hashes are one way functions. They take a message and turn it into gibberish. It's almost impossible to reverse a hash back to the original message. Hashes are used to create special strings for encryption, and to verify the message was unaltered during transmission. Some hashes are stronger than others. Type some text in the box below to see hashes computed in realtime. Hover over the hash names for more detail.

randomness.

Randomness is vital to the creation of secret (and very important!) keys for encryption and decryption. Crypto is extremely reliant upon the use of numbers with near perfect randomness. If one's random number source is not random, the resulting keys will enable the enemy to mathematically guess the secret keys! In this web application, we will use atmospheric noise to create a random "salt". A salt, is a string of characters mixed into the blender, along with one's secret message and secret key to make life miserable for cryptanalysts.

The button below is connected to a website which sucks down atmospheric noise. A near perfect source for random numbers for our salt. Press it multiple times for new numbers!

keys.

Keys are random strings of characters stuffed into an encryption algorithm along with your super secret message and salt. The key, the salt and your message are churned together via mathematical black magic and computational LSD trips. The resultant ciphertext should be indistinguishable from noise. Of all things crypto, the key is the numero uno most important item to protect. NEVER, EVER, EVER, NEVER, EVER give out details of your keys.

In modern cryptosystems, key lengths in symmetric encryption are typically between 64bits to 512bits. The longer the key, the stronger the key is to attack. Longer keys also take longer to calculate. The three buttons below combine two things: a password and random noise(salt). These two items are pushed through a key expansion algorithm called PBKDF2. This creates a long, super strong key from relatively small sources of data. Be patient, the PBKDF2 runs 1000 times per key created! Open your browser's Javascript console for extra detail.

encryption.

Ok, we are now at the fun part! Encryption is the process of turning one's secret message, and secret key into a blob of unusable, unreadable binary goo. Only the owners of the secret key can unlock the secret message. There are a number of encryption algorithms out there; some better than others. Below we will play with a couple of the modern ones. All of these are considered fairly safe from prying eyes. In fact, AES with a 512 bit key is allowed to encrypt upto the SECRET level in military communications. That's pretty darn good.
